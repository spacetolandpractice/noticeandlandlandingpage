What is this?

A place to pause.
One small check-in a week.

Notice & Land is a printable PDF tracker that asks you to slow down for a few minutes each week and simply notice — what's happening in your body, your emotional landscape, your energy. Nothing to fix. Nothing to analyse.

It's grounded in nervous system awareness: the understanding that before we can shift how we feel, we need to know where we actually are. This resource gives you a gentle, structured way to do that — week by week, without pressure.

Think of it as a quiet ritual. Something that's just for you.

Why this matters

Your nervous system is
running the show.

When you're in survival mode — and early motherhood is survival mode — your body and brain are working hard to keep you upright. There's very little left over for reflection, for processing, for feeling.

The nervous system isn't just about stress. It shapes how safe you feel, how connected you feel, how available you are — to your baby, to yourself, to anything. And most of the time, in motherhood, nobody asks us to check in with it at all.

Noticing is not fixing.

You don't have to change anything to use this tool. The act of naming where you are is enough. Awareness itself creates a kind of space — even a few millimetres of it.

The body holds information.

Before emotions make it to words, they live in the body. Tension in your jaw. Heaviness in your chest. This tracker starts there — in sensation — and works gently upward.

Weekly is enough.

This isn't a daily practice. It's a weekly pause. Five to ten minutes, once a week — enough to stay in touch with yourself without adding to your load.

It's just for you.

No sharing required. No output. Nothing to show anyone. This exists in the quiet space between who you're being for everyone else and who you actually are.

Who made this

Hi, I'm Meg.

I'm a qualified counsellor and the founder of Space to Land — a practice built specifically for mothers navigating the enormity of early motherhood and matrescence.

I created Notice & Land because I kept seeing the same thing in my work with mothers: not a lack of resilience, not a failure to cope — but a complete disconnection from themselves. Women who had been so busy holding everything together that they'd stopped noticing what they were actually feeling. This resource is my attempt to give that back, gently and without pressure.

I know what it is to be in the thick of it. And I know that sometimes the most useful thing isn't advice — it's just a quiet place to land.
